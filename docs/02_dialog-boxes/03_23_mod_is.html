Instantaneous sampling (IS)#

Space-to-event (STE)

Instantaneous sampling (IS) (Moeller et al., 2018): A method used to estimate abundance or density from time-lapse images from randomly deployed cameras; the number of unique individuals (the count) is needed (Moeller et al., 2018).

Assumptions, Pros, Cons
Assumptions

  • Demographic closure (i.e., no births or deaths) (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Geographic closure (i.e., no immigration or emigration) (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Camera locations are randomly placed (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Detections are independent (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Detection is perfect (detection probability ‘p’ = 1) (Moeller et al., 2018)

Pros

  • Can be efficient for estimating abundance of common species (with a lot of images) (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Flexible assumption of animals’ distribution (Moeller et al., 2018)

Cons

  • Requires accurate counts of animals (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Assumes that perfect (detection probability ‘p’ = 1) (Moeller et al., 2018)

  • Reduced precision (Moeller et al., 2018)

The instantaneous sampling model (IS) is an extension of the space-to-event model (STE; see Space-to-event (STE)) that uses counts of animals in time-lapse images – instead of the area until an animal is first detected – to estimate density (Moeller et al., 2018). As with the STE, all cameras in a randomly-deployed array are programmed to take time-lapse images at predefined intervals (e.g., every hour) to get instantaneous “snapshot” samples of the study area. During image processing, the number of animals in each photograph is recorded. Thus, the IS is essentially a series of fixed-area point counts (Moeller et al., 2018): camera traps act as “standing observers” tabulating the number of individuals seen within a set area and time.

Simulations and Field Experiments

The IS is relatively untested opposite its sister models. Simulations have shown that the IS is unbiased to animal movement speed or population size, so is applicable to slow- and fast-moving animals and to low- and high-density populations (Moeller et al., 2018). When tested on a population of elk in Idaho, the IS produced a similar density estimate as an aerial survey, but which was less precise than both TTE- and STE-derived estimates (Moeller et al., 2018).
