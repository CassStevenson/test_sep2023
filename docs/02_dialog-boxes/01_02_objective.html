Species diversity vs Richness

Note

This tool currently provides one set of recommendations for Species diversity & richness; we hope to expand this in the future to provide recommendations more fine-tuned to Richness vs. Diversity as well as the as multiple “levels” described below.

Species diversity: A measure of diversity that incorporates both the number of species in an assemblage and some measure of their relative abundances.’ (Gotelli & Chao, 2013)

Species richness: The total number of species in an assemblage or a sample’ (Gotelli & Chao, 2013).

Pyron (2010) – Fig. 1 Species evenness and species richness for animalcule communities. Both communities contain five species of animalcules. Species richness is the same. The community on the left is dominated by one of the species. The community on the right has equal proportions of each species. Evenness is higher when species are present in similar proportions. Thus the community on the left has higher species diversity, because evenness is higher.

Note that there are multiple “levels” to Species diversity & richness, these include:

  • Alpha richness (α): The number of species at the level of an individual camera location (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

  • Beta-diversity (β): The differences between the communities or, more formally, the variance among the communities (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

  • Gamma richness (γ): The number of species across a whole study area (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

(Babu, N.D.)

Species inventory (presence) vs. Occupancy

Species inventory: Rapid assessment surveys used to determine what species are present in a given area at a given point in time; there is no attempt made to quantify aspects of communities or populations (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

Occupancy: The probability a site is occupied by the species (MacKenzie et al., 2002). Occupancy is also highly suitable for evaluating broad-scale patterns of species distribution (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

Absolute abundance vs. Relative abundance vs. Density

Absolute abundance: The number of individuals in a population (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

Relative abundance: The number of animals detected from one camera/area compared to another camera/area.

Vital rates

Vital rate: The species-specific factors of a population that, together, play a large role in the population’s trend. These include the birth rate, recruitment rate, and probability of survival and mortality.’ NBCKC / CNSCB, 2024

Behaviour

Behaviour: behaviour focused objectives vary greatly; they may be qualitative or quantitative (e.g., diel activity patterns, mating, boldness, predation, foraging, activity patterns, vigilance, parental care (Caravaggi et al., 2020; Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017).

Objective vs. State Variable

One of the first choices you will make when designing your study, is what it is that you plan to measure (or your “state variable”; a formal measure that summarizes the state of a community or population at a particular time [Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017]). Since this concept might be easy to confuse with Survey Objective(s) (and since “objective” is often referred to interchangeably with “state variable”), consider the following figure:

What should objectives include?

To expand upon the figure above, should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound (i.e., SMART). should describe the following:

  • Target Species - the species that the is designed to detect,

  • - a formal measure that summarizes the state of a community or population at a particular time (Wearn & Glover-Kapfer, 2017) (e.g., species richness or population abundance), and

  • Proposed modelling approach(es) - the method used to analyze the camera data, which should depend on the (e.g., occupancy models [MacKenzie et al., 2002], spatially explicit capture-recapture (SECR) models [e.g., ] for density estimation, etc.) and the Target Species.

An example of a clearly defined could be “to monitor trends in wolverine occupancy at 5-year intervals from March – December 2020 to 2030 in wildlife management unit 539”.

The Survey Objective will determine the appropriate study design and deployment considerations (e.g., camera spacing, effort, attractants or not).
